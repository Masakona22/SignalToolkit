2.1.5. asymmetric encryption

Uses of asymmetric cryptography

Asymmetric cryptography is typically used to authenticate data using digital signatures. A digital signature is a mathematical technique used to validate the authenticity and integrity of a message, software or digital document. It is the digital equivalent of a handwritten signature or stamped seal. Based on asymmetric cryptography, digital signatures can provide assurances of evidence to the origin, identity and status of an electronic document, transaction or message, as well as acknowledge informed consent by the signer. Asymmetric cryptography can also be applied to systems in which many users may need to encrypt and decrypt messages, including:

  • Encrypted email: A public key can be used to encrypt a message and a private key can be used to decrypt it.
  • Secure browsing: Asymmetric cryptography is used to establish a secure connection over an insecure network, such as the internet.
  • Secure shell (SSH): SSH is a cryptographic network protocol that uses asymmetric cryptography to authenticate users and machines and encrypt the information that passes between them.
  • Virtual private networks (VPNs): VPNs use asymmetric cryptography to authenticate users and encrypt information that passes between them.
  • Cryptocurrencies: Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies rely on asymmetric cryptography. Users have public keys that everyone can see and private keys that are kept secret. Bitcoin uses a cryptographic algorithm to ensure only legitimate owners can spend the funds.

    • What are the benefits and disadvantages of asymmetric cryptography?

    The benefits of asymmetric cryptography include:

  • Security is increased since the private keys don't ever have to be transmitted or revealed to anyone.
  • The private keys can be kept on a separate device, such as a smart card, and never be exposed to the network.
  • The private keys can be used to create digital signatures that verify the authenticity of messages.
  • The key distribution problem is eliminated because there's no need for exchanging keys.


    • The disadvantages of asymmetric cryptography include:

  • It's slower than symmetric cryptography.
  • It's more computationally intensive than symmetric cryptography.
  • It's more difficult to implement.
  • Because public keys aren't authenticated, no one can ensure a public key belongs to the person specified. Consequently, users must verify that their public keys belong to them.
  • If a malicious actor identifies a person's private key, the attacker can read that individual's messages.
